Shams Dubai & DEWA net metering, explained

A plain-language guide to going solar in Dubai: what the Shams Dubai programme is, how net metering credits your bill, which equipment DEWA will approve, and the realistic timelines from first enquiry to a live system.

What is Shams Dubai

Shams Dubai is the Dubai Electricity & Water Authority's (DEWA) programme for grid-tied rooftop solar. It lets owners of villas, commercial buildings and industrial sites install solar panels that work alongside the DEWA grid rather than replacing it. Your panels generate electricity during the day; whatever your property does not use at that moment flows back to the grid, and DEWA credits it against what you draw at night.

Because the system stays connected to the grid, you keep an uninterrupted supply and never manage your own generation manually. You can read the official programme details on the DEWA Shams Dubai page. Every connection is metered, approved and inspected by DEWA, which is why the choice of equipment and the approval steps matter — both are covered below.

How net metering works

When you go solar under Shams Dubai, DEWA replaces your standard meter with a bi-directional smart meter. It records two separate figures: the energy you import from the grid and the energy you export back to it when your panels produce more than you are using.

  • Surplus is credited at the retail rate. Units you export are valued at the same tariff you would have paid to import them — so every exported unit offsets a future imported unit one-for-one.
  • Credits roll over month to month. If you export more than you import in a given month, the surplus is carried forward as a credit against later months rather than being lost.
  • Net credits carry into the next year. Any remaining net credit at the year's end rolls over, which smooths out the seasonal swing between long summer cooling loads and lighter winter demand.
  • It is a bill credit, not a cash payout. Net metering reduces what you owe DEWA; it does not pay you cash for surplus generation. The financial return comes from a smaller bill, not a cheque.

In practice this means a well-sized system quietly works down your monthly DEWA bill, with the meter doing the accounting automatically in the background.

Eligible Equipment — models, not brands

DEWA maintains an online Eligible Equipment registry of the solar modules and inverters permitted under Shams Dubai. Two points are widely misunderstood and worth being precise about:

  • DEWA approves specific models, not entire brands. A manufacturer having some registered products does not mean every product it sells is eligible. The exact module and inverter models on your proposal must each appear on the registry.
  • The registry is continuously updated. It is a live list, not a fixed PDF — models are added and revised over time, so the right check is always against the current registry, not last year's version.

On the module side, DEWA-registered brands include names such as JinkoSolar, LONGi, Trina Solar, JA Solar, AIKO Energy, Canadian Solar and Waaree Energies, among others. On the inverter side you will see brands such as Huawei, Sungrow, GoodWe, Solis, SMA and Fronius. PV module product and performance warranties typically run 25 to 30 years, while inverter warranties are usually 5 to 10 years. We only propose equipment whose exact models are on the current registry, and we confirm eligibility before anything is ordered.

Grid-tied vs backup power

This is the single most important thing to understand before you buy, because it is often assumed the wrong way round. A standard Shams Dubai installation is grid-tied: it is designed to run in parallel with the DEWA grid and to lower your bill, not to keep the lights on during a power cut.

A grid-tied system without a battery does not provide backup during a grid outage. For safety, the inverter automatically disconnects when the grid goes down — so if DEWA supply is interrupted, your solar system also stops, even in bright sunshine. Backup power requires a separate battery system (an energy storage system, or ESS), which is a different design and a different investment.

For most Dubai properties, where grid supply is highly reliable, a grid-tied system without storage is the right economic choice: it captures the full bill-reduction benefit of net metering at the lowest cost per watt. If uninterrupted supply through outages is a genuine requirement for your site, we can scope an ESS separately — but it should be a deliberate decision, not an assumption.

The approval process & realistic timelines

Every Shams Dubai connection goes through DEWA's approval and inspection process. We will not promise a fixed number of days, because the honest answer depends on site size and DEWA scheduling. Instead, here are the transparent per-stage targets we work to:

  • Pre-calculation — within 24 hours. From your recent DEWA bill we model an illustrative savings and payback range so you can decide whether to proceed.
  • Free engineering assessment — up to 3 days. A site visit and roof/electrical survey to confirm the design, the exact eligible models, and firm figures.
  • DEWA filing — within roughly 5 days of signing. We prepare and submit the Shams Dubai application on your behalf once the design is agreed.
  • Installation — 2 to 7 days on site for a typical villa, once approvals and equipment are in place.
  • Typical villa cycle — 45 to 60 days from first enquiry to a live, metered system, allowing for DEWA approval and inspection windows.

Larger sites naturally take longer. As a guide to the full cycle by system size:

  • Villa (5–20 kWp): approximately 4–8 weeks.
  • Small commercial (20–100 kWp): approximately 6–10 weeks.
  • Warehouse (100–500 kWp): approximately 2–4 months.
  • Industrial (>500 kWp): approximately 4–8 months.

Tariffs — and why solar is cheaper

The economics of Shams Dubai come down to a simple comparison: what a grid unit costs you versus what a self-generated solar unit costs. DEWA residential tariffs are banded by consumption — illustratively 23, 28, 32 and 38 fils per kWh across the slabs — plus a fuel surcharge of around 6 fils per kWh, with 5% VAT on top. That puts the effective all-in grid rate for most households in the region of 0.29 to 0.44 AED per kWh. Commercial supply is charged at a flat rate of around 38 fils plus the surcharge, working out near 0.44 AED per kWh.

Self-generated solar, by contrast, works out at roughly 0.11 AED per kWh on a levelised basis over the life of the system — because once the panels are installed, the sunlight is free and the ongoing cost is minimal. Every unit your roof produces displaces a grid unit that would have cost two to four times as much. That gap, applied across the ~1,750 kWh a well-oriented kilowatt of panels generates each year in Dubai, is what drives the savings and payback figures.
